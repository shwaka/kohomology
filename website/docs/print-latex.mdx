## Why LaTeX?
According to [this page](./free-loop-space),
you can define the Sullivan model of the free loop space $LS^2$ of the 2-sphere $S^2$
as follows:

<ImportKotlin path="PrintTex.kt" restrict="def" />

Now you can compute and print the basis of the cohomology $H^*(LS^2)$ by

<ImportKotlin path="PrintTex.kt" restrict="plain" />

and this prints

```
H^0(LS^2) = Q[[1]]
H^1(LS^2) = Q[[sx]]
H^2(LS^2) = Q[[x]]
H^3(LS^2) = Q[[sxsy]]
H^4(LS^2) = Q[[1/2 xsy + ysx]]
H^5(LS^2) = Q[[sxsy^2]]
H^6(LS^2) = Q[[1/4 xsy^2 + ysxsy]]
H^7(LS^2) = Q[[sxsy^3]]
H^8(LS^2) = Q[[1/6 xsy^3 + ysxsy^2]]
H^9(LS^2) = Q[[sxsy^4]]
H^10(LS^2) = Q[[1/8 xsy^4 + ysxsy^3]]
```

But this output is not very readable since

- `xsy` can be confused with the product of three indeterminates `x`, `s` and `y`.
- Fractions such as `1/8` are hard to read.

## Solution

By using `Printer`, you can print a LaTeX code:

<ImportKotlin path="PrintTex.kt" restrict="tex" />

```latex
H^{0}(LS^2) &= \Q[[1]] \\
H^{1}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\bar{x}]] \\
H^{2}(LS^2) &= \Q[[{x}]] \\
H^{3}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\bar{x}\bar{y}]] \\
H^{4}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\frac{1}{2} {x}\bar{y} + {y}\bar{x}]] \\
H^{5}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\bar{x}\bar{y}^{2}]] \\
H^{6}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\frac{1}{4} {x}\bar{y}^{2} + {y}\bar{x}\bar{y}]] \\
H^{7}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\bar{x}\bar{y}^{3}]] \\
H^{8}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\frac{1}{6} {x}\bar{y}^{3} + {y}\bar{x}\bar{y}^{2}]] \\
H^{9}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\bar{x}\bar{y}^{4}]] \\
H^{10}(LS^2) &= \Q[[\frac{1}{8} {x}\bar{y}^{4} + {y}\bar{x}\bar{y}^{3}]] \\
```
